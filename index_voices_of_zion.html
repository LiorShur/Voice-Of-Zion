History · Land · People

From Ancient Israel to Modern Israel

The Jewish people emerged in this land more than 2,000 years ago and maintained a continuous presence, prayer, and longing for Zion through exile, return, and renewal.

Ancient Israelites in the Land of Israel

Historians identify the ancient Israelites as originating in the area of today’s Israel and surrounding regions well before 1000 BCE, forming kingdoms of Israel and Judah and centering their national and spiritual life in Jerusalem and Zion.

A Continuous Jewish Presence

Even after conquests, exiles, and foreign rule, Jewish communities remained in cities like Jerusalem, Safed, Hebron, and Tiberias. Modern research on 19th-century censuses shows a significant Jewish population in the Land of Israel before the modern Zionist movement.

Zionism: National Home in the Historical Homeland

Modern Zionism arose in the late 19th century as a movement for Jewish self-determination and return to the ancestral homeland, especially after centuries of persecution and, later, the trauma of the Holocaust. It combined an ancient spiritual attachment with a modern struggle for safety, dignity, and democracy.

Historical & legal milestones

League of Nations Mandate for Palestine

In 1922, the League of Nations confirmed the British Mandate over Palestine with a clear responsibility: to establish a “Jewish national home” while safeguarding the civil and religious rights of all inhabitants, regardless of race or religion.

Read the Mandate text →

UN Partition Plan: Two States for Two Peoples

In 1947, the UN General Assembly recommended partitioning the land into independent Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under an international regime. The Jewish leadership accepted the plan; Arab leaders rejected it, leading to war after Israel’s declaration of independence in 1948.

Read UN Resolution 181 →

Israel’s Declaration of Independence

Israel’s founding document, proclaimed in 1948, affirms the Jewish people’s historic connection to the land and commits the state to equality of social and political rights for all its inhabitants, irrespective of religion, race, or sex, and to the pursuit of peace with its neighbors.

People, Not Just Politics

For Jews worldwide, “Zion” is not only a political concept. It appears in daily prayers, songs, poetry, and ritual – expressing a deep spiritual and emotional bond to Jerusalem and the Land of Israel that has lasted for millennia.

Jewish Nobel Laureates

Jews are about 0.2% of the world’s population, yet over 20% of individual Nobel Prize laureates are Jewish or of Jewish descent – contributing to physics, medicine, chemistry, economics, literature, and peace.

Innovation & Start-up Nation

Israel is known as the “Start-up Nation” for its high concentration of tech start-ups and R&D. It has led advances in cybersecurity, medical devices, water technology, and more, with support from national innovation programs.

Zionism’s Core Values

At its heart, Zionism is the movement for the Jewish people’s right to self-determination and safety in their ancestral homeland. It does not require denying the rights of others. Many Zionists see a just future as one where Israelis and Palestinians live in peace, security, and dignity.

Against Antisemitism & Dehumanization

Criticism of any government’s policies is legitimate. But when rhetoric denies the Jewish people’s right to self-determination, erases their history in this land, or targets Jews anywhere simply for being Jewish, it crosses into antisemitism and harms hopes for peace.

Frequently Asked Questions

Demonstrations can be loud and emotional. This FAQ is meant to give short, calm answers and point you back to the basic ideas on this page: history, rights, and human dignity for everyone.

Does supporting Zionism mean I am against Palestinians?
No. Zionism is the movement for the Jewish people’s right to self-determination and safety in their ancestral homeland. Many people support both a secure Jewish state and real self-determination and dignity for Palestinians. Wanting safety and freedom for one people should not mean denying it to another.
Did Jews suddenly “arrive” here in 1948?
The Jewish people trace their origins to this land thousands of years ago. There were ancient kingdoms of Israel and Judah here, and Jewish communities have lived in places like Jerusalem, Safed, Hebron, and Tiberias across the centuries. Modern immigration and Israel’s independence added new waves of return, but they did not create the connection – they built on it.
Can I care about Palestinian suffering and still support Israel’s right to exist?
Yes. Caring about Palestinian lives, rights, and freedom is compatible with recognizing the Jewish people’s right to a safe national home in their historic homeland. Many Israelis and Jews around the world believe that the only moral and realistic future is one where both peoples live in security, dignity, and mutual recognition.
Is it antisemitic to criticize the Israeli government?
Criticizing any government’s policies – including Israel’s – is legitimate. It becomes antisemitic when it denies the Jewish people’s right to self-determination, uses classic antisemitic stereotypes, blames all Jews for the actions of a state, or calls for the elimination of Israel as a Jewish homeland. The line is between policy critique and dehumanizing a people.
Do Arabs and other minorities have rights in Israel?
Israel’s Declaration of Independence promises full social and political rights to all inhabitants, irrespective of religion, race, or sex. Arab citizens of Israel vote, have political parties and representatives in parliament, serve as judges, doctors, academics, and more. There are real debates and challenges about equality in practice – but the legal framework recognizes minority rights and political participation.
